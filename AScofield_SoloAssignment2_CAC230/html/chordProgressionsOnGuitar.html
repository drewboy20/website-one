Using Chord Progressions on Guitar













Old Faithful: 12 Bar Blues

If you remember from the home page, one of the most simple chord progressions you can have is a I-IV-V. The progression is found in early spiritual gospel songs. And blues, rising out of the Mississippi Delta, was heavily influenced by the style. 12 Bar refers to the fact that the chord progressions last 12 measures of musical notation before starting the progression all over again.

Now with a little bit of background, we can begin dissecting structure. The first thing we will learn is the basics of a chord progression.




As you can see and hear, the 12 bar blues progression is not too difficult to play or understand.

Now that we have a solid chord base, let's try and see if we can add some more layers. I am sure one of the first images that comes to mind when I say, "blues player," is a lead guitarist making a grimacing face as he bends the guitar strings to make an ear-piercing, emotional note. What you are hearing the guitar player doing is playing a solo. A solo is where a lead guitar player plays a series of notes in a scale, usually in improvisation, to display his virtuosity or musical feel. Soloing is a skill that takes years to develop and oftentimes only a few players out of thousands have enough musical wherewithal to make them sound good. So what musical scale works best at what time?
It all depends on the feel the player is trying to convey In the case of the blues, a minor scale usually works best to convey the sadness and heartbreak that is the usual theme of the blues. And blues players will often use a different scale than the diatonic scale. Instead of the seven notes that are in each octave of the diatonic scale, there is another scale that uses five. This is the pentatonic scale. The pentatonic is the diatonic scale, but without the 4th and 7th intervals.

← So how do you play this on the guitar? Guitar players will usually memorize a shape on the fretboard. →

Within this shape lies all the notes in a minor pentatonic scale (more than once). This shape can be moved up and down the fretboard depending on what key you are in. Here is an example of me playing with this scale over the previous 12 bar blues audio:



You might have noticed that I wasn't exactly playing entirely inside the shape, that's because I was using what is called an extension. An extension uses the same notes, but in different places. This is good for if you need to add movement to your solo without having to move all the way up to a different part of the fretboard.
