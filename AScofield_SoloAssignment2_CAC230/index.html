Your Guide to Music Listening

Are you tired of hearing what seems like the same songs playing over and over again on the radio?

"This song sounds just like the last one!"

"I can't seem to find MY genre!"

"I want to hear something unexpected!"

If so, you are in need of a new set of tools to go inside your temporal lobe toolbox. A new dimension of knowledge and texture to open your third ear. What I hope to briefly teach you is a basic guide to identifying structures of popular songs to make your experience in listening to them greatly better.

The Basics

First, before we can go anywhere, we must first make sure we know a little bit about the basics of music.

So, to begin, there are basic notes and scales-- we'll start with the most popular scale: the diatonic scale. 'Dia', meaning 'through,' and 'tonic'--meaning, unexpectedly and surprisingly--'tone,' refers to the types of whole-note scales that we all know and love. The definition of whole-tone can be likened to how there are whole and decimal point number systems. For music, the relative hertz frequency difference determines what notes are whole steps apart. The diatonic scale features seven notes per octave. The classic Do-Re-Mi-Fa-Sol-La-Ti-Do scale is one example of a popular whole-tone major (or happier sounding) scale that most of us are familiar with.

And as you can see, each sound in the Do-Re-Mi scale is related to a note--in this case of the C major scale. Now, as a side note (no pun intended), not every major diatonic scale has notes that are not sharp (#) or flat (♭) after it (ex. F#), the E major scale is E, F#, G#, A, B, C#, and D#, but as mentioned, it is all about having an equal relative hertz frequency between each note that determines whether certain notes are part of any whole-tone scale.

Song Structure

Now with a little bit of background, we can begin dissecting structure. The first thing we will learn is the basics of a chord progression.

So what is a chord progression?

As you might guess, it is the way a song is sequentially structured by way of musical chords. Chord progressions are the spine of most music you will hear and have been standardized in Western music. So how do they work? Let's say we add numbers to the notes we're talking about, C-G. So, the first note (also known as the root note), C, let's label that with 1. D with 2. E with 3, and so on until we get to B equaling 7 (the top C would be 1 again). What you would have is something like this:

Now, if you want to make a song, you can use those numbers to mark the chords you want to use. A common chord progression is 1-4-5, or in C major C-F-G. In the music world, the symbols to mark a chord progression are actually roman numeral. So 1-4-5 would be I-IV-V. Here are some songs that use the I-IV-V chord progression:

  • Amazing Grace
  • Lucy in the Sky with Diamonds - The Beatles
  • Sweet Home Alabama - Lynyrd Skynyrd
  • Good Riddance - Green Day

As you can see, the I-IV-V progression was very popular in the early rock era and even in early American gospel songs. Now we can look at a complex variation. To add another layer, lowercase roman numerals mean that the chord is minor, and UPPERCASE roman numerals meaning that the chord is MAJOR. A very popular chord progression I-V-vi-IV and its near relative vi–IV–I–V. If we are in the key of C, this would mean C-G-Aminor-F.

Songs that use the I-IV-V chord progression:

  • Amazing Grace
  • Lucy in the Sky with Diamonds - The Beatles
  • Sweet Home Alabama - Lynyrd Skynyrd
  • Good Riddance - Green Day

Songs that use the I-V-vi-IV chord progression:

  • Let it Be - The Beatles
  • Under the Bridge - Red Hot Chili Peppers
  • Photograph - Ed Sheeran

Songs that use the vi-IV-I-V chord progression:

  • The Scientist - Coldplay
  • All of Me - John Legend
  • Despacito - Luis Fonsi
